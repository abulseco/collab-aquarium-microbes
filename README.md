## Abstract
Microbial communities are highly responsive to environmental disturbances and can exhibit rapid shifts in community composition and function following perturbation. Interventions in marine aquaria, such as antiparasitic treatments, may trigger cascading effects on non-target microbial taxa, which have implications for ecosystem stability, resilience, and overall function. Here, we explore how microbial community structure relates to antiparasitic strategies and aquarium health. We accomplished this through two separate projects analyzing 16S rRNA (bacterial) and 18S rRNA (eukaryotic) datasets. First, we monitored microbial dynamics associated with a therapeutic copper sulfate treatment targeting Cryptocaryon irritans. Second, we analyzed a longer-term time series with multiple treatments administered in various combinations (praziquantel, copper, and salinity adjustments) to manage infections of Neobenedenia sp. and C. irritans. We found that microbial community structure and diversity responded to antiparasitic treatments in both the copper sulfate and time series experiments with temporal modeling showing treatment-specific trends. Changes in the bacterial community structure were also associated with trends in putative metabolic functions, particularly those related to heterotrophic metabolism and nitrification. In addition, we identified taxa associated with treatment and disease status using random forest modeling, highlighting potential microbial indicators of system condition. Detection of C. irritans in the 18S rRNA dataset corresponded with visual diagnosis of outbreaks, whereas Neobenedenia sp. was not detected despite visual confirmation on fish. Together, these findings demonstrate that antiparasitic management strategies can drive treatment-specific changes in aquarium microbial communities, with implications for system stability and disease monitoring.
